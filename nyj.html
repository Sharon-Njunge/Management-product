NYJ SUMMARY 1 & 2

In born a crime by Trevor Noah, the opening chapters introduce key themes and issues that resonate with
Kiambu county. In chapter one, Noah explores his childhood in apartheid-era South Africa, where his existence
as the biracial child of a black Xhosa mother and a white Swiss father was illegal under the oppressive regime.
This theme for racial segregation and identity carries weight in Kiambu county where ethnicity and social
status can deeply influence one’s opportunities and treatment within society.

Chapter 2 delves into Noah's experiences navigating the complexities of race and identity, particularly the challenges
he faced in establishing connections with both black and white relatives. Similarly, in Kiambu county, the legacy of
colonialism and ethnic divisions can create barriers to social cohesion among diverse communities

Moreover, Noah’s anecdotes about his mother’s resilience and ingenuity in the face of adversity resonate with the
spirit of many individuals in Kiambu county, where resourcefulness and determination are essential for survival and
progress.

Additionally, the themes of family dynamics and cultural heritage explored in these chapters reflect the
importance of community and tradition in Kiambu county, where familial ties and ancestral customs play
a significant role in shaping daily life.

Overall the themes of race, identity, resilience, and community depicted in the opening chapters of
Born a crime parallel the social, cultural, and historical landscape of Kiambu county, providing insight
into the shared struggles and triumphs of individuals navigating complex systems of power and prejudice.
